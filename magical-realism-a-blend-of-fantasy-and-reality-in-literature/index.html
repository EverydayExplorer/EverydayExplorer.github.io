Magical Realism: A Blend of Fantasy and Reality in Literature

3 min · 612 words

Magical Realism is a unique genre of literature which blends the magical and fantastical elements with realistic situations, settings, and characters. It is not a fantasy genre but is a literary style that highlights the paradoxes and ambivalent characteristics of the real world through the inclusion of supernatural and magical elements. Magical Realism is a sophisticated form of writing that takes an imaginative approach to reality and juxtaposes it with the magical. It is a literary style that has gained immense popularity among the readers and writers alike, and it is often used as a tool to capture the essence of various cultural aspects.

Origins of Magical Realism:

Magical Realism as a literary genre originated in Latin America in the early 20th century, specifically with the works of Gabriel Garcia Marquez, Jorge Luis Borges and Isabel Allende. They were inspired by the rich history, legends, and myths of their cultures, and sought out to use it to create a new style of writing. They incorporated magical and supernatural elements in their stories but conveyed them in a realistic manner. They used magical realism to create an alternate reality within the real world and explore the ideas of cultural identity, history, and politics.

What are the Characteristics of Magical Realism?

Magical Realistic works have numerous defining characteristics. Some of the significant ones are:

1. Blending of Fact and Fantasy:

Magical Realistic writers fuse the fantastic and the real; the ordinary and the extraordinary. They use magical elements to present the real-world situations in a unique angle, making it surreal, but they never forget to anchor their stories with a realistic setting and backdrop.

2. Complex Characters:

Magical Realistic characters are often complex creatures who evade identification or explanation. They are often characters who are caught in a conflict between the real and the magical world. They are characters who embody the ambiguity and mystery of the genre.

3. Time and Distance are Flexible:

Magical Realism disregards the conventional linear timeline of events. It plays with time and distance, making them flexible. It jumps from one time period to another, from one location to another, making the story a dream-like surrealism.

4. Multiple Narrators:

Magical Realistic works rarely follow a single narrator perspective. They often have multiple narrators telling the story from their thought process and angles, making the narrative vibrant and complex.

5. Strong Cultural Foundation:

Magical realism is inherently linked to the culture and identity of its creator. The cultural element plays a vital role in most of the Magical Realistic writings, and it often explores the various social and political hierarchies deeply embedded in culture that are problematic.

The Evolution of Magical Realism:

Magical Realism has grown in popularity in the publishing industry over the last few decades, with numerous books such as ‘The Night Circus’ by Erin Morgenstern, ‘The Ocean at the End of the Lane’ by Neil Gaiman, and ‘The Invisible Life of Addie LaRue’ by V.E. Schwab gaining widespread acclaim. The genre has even found its way into TV shows and movies like ‘American Gods,’ ‘Pan’s Labyrinth,’ and ‘Midnight’s Children.’

Conclusion:

In conclusion, Magical Realism is a unique style of literature which blends the real and the surreal seamlessly. It is a genre that encourages readers to explore the complexities and dichotomies inherent in the real world through the lens of the fantastical. Magical Realism has a strong cultural foundation and provides a voice to the marginalised, providing insights into cultural identity and practices. It is a literary style that has evolved considerably over the decades, and it continues to influence modern literature, film, and television, captivating readers and viewers worldwide.
