Exploring the Symbolism in To\ Kill\ a\ Mockingbird

4 min · 841 words

In this celebrated novel, the author delves into the deep-rooted prejudices and the socio-cultural divides prevalent in the Southern States of America during the 1930s, through the eyes of a child. The story that unfolds attempts to showcase the best and worst of humanity, and in doing so, explores a wide range of complex themes and ideas.

One of the most important literary techniques employed by the author is the usage of symbolism. Symbolism is the use of objects, characters, or actions to represent abstract concepts or ideas, and is a powerful tool in creative writing. The symbols used in this book range from the mockingbird to the tree with a knot representing prejudice, and many others.

The Mockingbird

One of the most prominent symbols in the book is the mockingbird. The mockingbird is used to represent innocence, and is a powerful symbol because of its association with singing, which is itself a symbol of beauty and purity. The mockingbird is also a creature that is defenseless against predators, and so its death is a senseless and brutal act. This symbol is used throughout the book, from the first chapter to the last, and becomes increasingly important as the story progresses.

The Radley Tree

Another important symbol in the book is the tree outside the Radley house, which has a knot in the trunk that is said to be the shape of a person. This knot is seen as a symbol of prejudice, since it is assumed that Mr. Radley created the knot to punish his son for some misdeed. The knot in the Radley tree is a powerful symbol because it represents the way that prejudice is passed down from one generation to the next, and how it can be ingrained in society to the point that it seems natural and unchangeable.

The Snowman

The snowman is another important symbol in the book. It is created by Jem and Scout, and is made to resemble Mr. Avery, a neighbor who is described as ’the meanest man ever God blew breath into.’ The creation of the snowman is a way for the children to express their feelings about Mr. Avery, and it is a symbol of the way that people can be demonized and dehumanized in order to justify prejudice and discrimination.

The Camellia

One of the lesser-known symbols in the book is the camellia bush in the yard of the Dubose house. Mrs. Dubose is an elderly woman who is addicted to morphine, and the camellia bush becomes a symbol of her struggle to overcome her addiction. As the children learn more about Mrs. Dubose, they come to see that she is a complex person who is fighting her own demons, and the camellia bush becomes a symbol of the struggle that many people face in overcoming addiction and other personal challenges.

Conclusion

‘To Kill a Mockingbird’ is a powerful work of literature that explores complex themes and ideas, many of which are represented through the use of symbolism. The mockingbird, the Radley tree, the snowman, and the camellia are just a few of the many symbols used in the book, and each one represents a different aspect of the human experience. By exploring these symbols, the author is able to convey powerful messages about prejudice, innocence, and the struggle for personal growth and understanding.
