The Pleiades Mythology: Stories of the Seven Sisters

4 min · 671 words

Across the celestial tapestry, the Pleiades star cluster, also known as the Seven Sisters, has captivated the imaginations of civilizations for millennia. These seven radiant stars have woven themselves into the fabric of mythology, inspiring countless stories and legends that have shaped our understanding of the cosmos.

The Myth of the Seven Sisters

In Greek mythology, the Pleiades were the seven daughters of Atlas, the Titan who bore the weight of the heavens on his shoulders, and Pleione, an Oceanid nymph. Their names, Maia, Electra, Taygete, Alcyone, Celaeno, Sterope, and Merope, each held a distinct significance.

Maia, the eldest, was the mother of Hermes, the messenger of the gods. Electra, her sister, was said to be the mother of Electra and Dardanus, the founders of Mycenae and Troy. Taygete was pursued by Zeus, and their union resulted in the birth of Lacedaemon, the legendary founder of Sparta.

Alcyone, the most prominent of the sisters, was associated with the halcyon bird. Legend has it that when she drowned in the sea, her husband, Ceyx, was transformed into the same bird and the two were reunited. Celaeno, known for her dark hair and stormy disposition, was said to be the mother of Lycus, the king of Thebes.

Sterope, the brightest of the Seven Sisters, was the mother of Oenomaus, the king of Pisa who challenged suitors to a chariot race. Merope, the faintest of the stars, was married to the mortal Sisyphus and was said to have hidden her face in shame due to her husband’s cunning nature.

The Pleiades in Ancient Cultures

The Pleiades have played a pivotal role in the calendars and navigation systems of various ancient cultures. For the Egyptians, the heliacal rising of the Pleiades, when they first appear above the horizon at dawn, marked the beginning of the new year.

In Babylonian astronomy, the Pleiades were known as ‘Subaru’ and were considered a harbinger of good fortune. The Japanese car manufacturer Subaru adopted this name in recognition of the cluster’s association with practical use and reliability.

The Pleiades in Modern Astronomy

Modern astronomy has revealed that the Pleiades are a young open star cluster located approximately 444 light-years from Earth. The stars are gravitationally bound together and share a common origin. The cluster is estimated to be around 100 million years old, making it one of the youngest in the Milky Way galaxy.

The Pleiades have become a valuable subject of scientific study, providing insights into the formation and evolution of stars. The cluster’s proximity to Earth allows astronomers to observe its individual members in detail, offering clues about the processes that shape stellar systems.

The Pleiades in Folklore and Literature

The Pleiades have left an enduring mark on folklore and literature throughout history. In Norse mythology, they were known as the ‘Seven Virgins’ or the ‘Seven Maidens’ and were associated with the goddess Freyja.

In Native American traditions, the Pleiades were often regarded as a symbol of fertility and abundance. The Hopi tribe of the American Southwest believed that the cluster represented the seven sisters who brought corn and other crops to their people.

The Pleiades have also inspired poets and writers for centuries. In William Wordsworth’s poem ‘The Seven Sisters,’ he describes the cluster as ‘fair as a flock of white doves.’ Robert Frost’s poem ‘The Pleiades’ explores the theme of loss and longing, using the stars as a metaphor for the passage of time and the fading of memories.

Conclusion

The Pleiades star cluster, with its captivating beauty and rich mythological tapestry, continues to enchant and inspire us today. From ancient civilizations to modern astronomers, the Seven Sisters have played a profound role in shaping our understanding of the cosmos. Their stories of love, loss, and celestial wonder will continue to captivate generations to come, reminding us of the enduring power of human imagination and the boundless mysteries that lie beyond our reach.
