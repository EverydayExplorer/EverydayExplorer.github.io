The Philosophy of Existential Literature: Questions of Life and Meaning

4 min · 841 words

Existential literature, with its probing inquiries into the nature of existence, serves as a mirror reflecting the fundamental questions that have preoccupied humanity for centuries. From the existentialist movement of the 20th century to contemporary works exploring themes of alienation, freedom, and the search for meaning, this genre of literature offers readers a rich tapestry of philosophical exploration and introspection.

At the heart of existential literature lies the existentialist philosophy, which asserts that human beings are fundamentally free and responsible for creating meaning in a seemingly indifferent universe. This philosophy challenges traditional notions of morality, religion, and societal norms, urging individuals to confront the inherent absurdity and ambiguity of existence.

One of the central themes explored in existential literature is the concept of authenticity—the idea of living in accordance with one’s true self and values, rather than conforming to external expectations or societal norms. Characters in existential novels often grapple with questions of identity and purpose, struggling to find meaning in a world that can seem devoid of inherent significance.

Take, for example, Albert Camus’ ‘The Stranger,’ a seminal work of existential literature that follows the story of Meursault, a detached and apathetic protagonist who confronts the absurdity of life in the face of his own impending death. Through Meursault’s existential journey, Camus invites readers to ponder the meaning of life and the consequences of living inauthentically.

Similarly, Franz Kafka’s works, such as ‘The Metamorphosis’ and ‘The Trial,’ delve into themes of alienation and the absurdity of bureaucratic systems. Kafka’s protagonists often find themselves trapped in incomprehensible situations, grappling with feelings of isolation and powerlessness in a world that defies rational explanation.

Existential literature also explores the concept of freedom—the notion that individuals have the power to shape their own destinies through the choices they make. However, this freedom comes with a heavy burden of responsibility, as characters must navigate the consequences of their actions in a world devoid of predetermined meaning.

In Jean-Paul Sartre’s ‘Nausea,’ protagonist Antoine Roquentin experiences a profound existential crisis upon realizing the inherent emptiness and meaninglessness of his existence. Through Roquentin’s existential journey, Sartre delves into questions of individual freedom and the search for authenticity in a world devoid of inherent meaning.

Ultimately, existential literature challenges readers to confront the fundamental questions of life and meaning, inviting them to embark on a journey of self-discovery and philosophical inquiry. By grappling with themes of authenticity, freedom, and the absurdity of existence, existential literature offers readers a powerful lens through which to explore the complexities of the human condition. So, the next time you pick up a work of existential literature, prepare to confront the existential questions that lie at the heart of the human experience.
