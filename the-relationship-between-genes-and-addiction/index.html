The Relationship between Genes and Addiction

3 min · 463 words

Addiction is a complex disease that affects millions of individuals worldwide. To understand addiction better, scientists have been researching the possibility of a genetic link for years. The human genome project, completed in 2003, revealed several genetic variations leading to addiction and substance use disorders. Consequently, addiction is not solely attributed to environmental factors or personal choices, but genetics are also influential.

Studies on twin subjects reveal a hereditary influence on addiction. If one identical twin had a substance abuse disorder, there was a high likelihood that the other would develop one as well. Nevertheless, in comparison to environmental factors, genetics contribute lesser to the overall risk of addiction. Addiction is a polygenic disease, which means it has multi-gene associations. Hence, having a certain gene does not guarantee addiction; instead, a combination of genetic variations results in the probability of developing an addiction.

The dopaminergic system in the brain is significant in matters relating to reward, pleasure, and addiction. The release of dopamine, also known as the ‘feel-good’ hormone, reinforces behavior and can link to addiction. The A1 allele variant in the DRD2 gene (dopamine receptor D2 gene) appears in most addiction cases. The gene variant leads to a decrease in dopamine receptor densities in the brain, increased craving, and reduced feelings of pleasure. As a result, the DRD2 A1 variant may make one more prone to addiction.

Another gene that influences addiction, among other behaviors, is the COMT gene. The gene codes for the enzyme catechol-o-methyltransferase, which regulates the concentrations of dopamine, adrenaline, and noradrenaline in the brain. The GG variant of the COMT gene is associated with a higher probability of addiction and impulsive behaviors. The variant damages the degradation of dopamine, leading to an increase in dopamine concentrations in the brain, leading to an overwhelming euphoric feeling.

Research has also revealed a link between addiction and the mu-opioid receptor genes. The mu-opioid receptor gene influences the regulation of the brain’s opioid systems, which regulate physiological functions such as pain, stress, and emotional regulation. Genetic variations in this gene may increase the likelihood of addiction, as substances such as opioids bind to the receptors, leading to pleasurable feelings and reinforcing the behavior.

ADH and ALDH genes result in the breakdown of alcohol in the body. As a result, people with genetic variants on these genes metabolize alcohol differently. The ADH1B gene variant leads to a reduction of alcohol breakdown, leading to an accumulation of acetaldehyde. The result is a decrease in pleasurable feelings during alcohol consumption, leading to a lower probability of addiction. Alternatively, the ADH1C gene variant leads to efficient alcohol breakdown, leading to pleasurable feelings and reinforcing behavior, increasing the probability of addiction.

In conclusion, genetics undoubtedly play a role in addiction, although the environmental, social, and neurological factors have an equal impact. Individual genetic variations reveal predispositions to addiction, yet several other genes also interact to influence the risk. The characteristics of polygenic diseases mean that detecting a ‘gene for addiction’ is not plausible. Nevertheless, by understanding the genetic variations involved in addiction, healthcare providers could better understand the risks faced by patients with addiction, potentially informing their treatment options. Improved understanding of genetic predispositions to addiction could ultimately decrease the risk and incidence of addiction.
