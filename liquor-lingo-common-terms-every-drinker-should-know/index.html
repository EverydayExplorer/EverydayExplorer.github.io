Liquor Lingo: Common Terms Every Drinker Should Know

4 min · 642 words

In the world of alcohol, there is a language all its own. From fancy cocktail bars to casual dive joints, there are certain terms and phrases that every drinker should be familiar with. Whether you’re a seasoned connoisseur or just starting to explore the world of spirits, understanding liquor lingo can enhance your drinking experience and impress your friends. In this blog post, we’ll explore some common terms that every drinker should know.

1. Neat: When ordering a drink ’neat,’ you’re asking for the spirit to be served straight from the bottle, without any mixers, ice, or garnishes. This is the purest way to experience the flavors of a particular liquor.

2. On the rocks: If you prefer your drink chilled but still want to savor the flavors, ordering a drink ‘on the rocks’ means that it will be served over ice. As the ice melts, it slightly dilutes the drink, creating a smoother taste.

3. Muddled: Muddling is a technique used in cocktail making to release the flavors of fruits, herbs, or other ingredients. By gently crushing the ingredients in the bottom of the glass with a muddler, you can extract their essence and infuse it into the drink.

4. Aperitif: An aperitif is a drink that is typically enjoyed before a meal to stimulate the appetite. These drinks are usually light, dry, and refreshing, such as a classic gin and tonic or a glass of sparkling wine.

5. Digestif: On the other hand, a digestif is a drink enjoyed after a meal to aid digestion. These drinks are often stronger and more complex in flavor, like a rich brandy or a smoky Scotch whisky.

6. Proof: In the United States, the alcoholic content of a spirit is measured in ‘proof.’ The proof number is double the percentage of alcohol by volume (ABV). For example, a spirit that is 80 proof is 40% ABV.

7. Dry: When a drink is described as ‘dry,’ it means that it has little to no sweetness. This term is often used for wines and cocktails, indicating a preference for less sugar.

8. Mixology: Mixology is the art and science of creating cocktails. A mixologist is someone who specializes in crafting unique and innovative drink recipes, often experimenting with different flavors, techniques, and ingredients.

9. Bitters: Bitters are concentrated herbal extracts infused with alcohol. They are used as a flavoring agent in cocktails to add complexity and balance. Common types of bitters include Angostura, Peychaud’s, and orange bitters.

10. Tasting notes: When discussing the flavors and aromas of a particular spirit, you might hear the term ’tasting notes.’ These are the descriptors used to capture the various elements of the drink, such as fruity, floral, smoky, or spicy.

11. Mixers: Mixers are non-alcoholic beverages used to dilute or enhance the flavors of spirits. Common mixers include soda water, tonic water, fruit juices, and cola. They can be used to create a wide range of cocktails and long drinks.

12. Highball vs. Lowball: These terms refer to the type of glassware used for a particular drink. A highball glass is tall and slender, typically used for drinks with a larger volume of mixer, like a gin and tonic. A lowball glass is shorter and wider, often used for spirits served on the rocks or with minimal mixers, like a whiskey neat or a classic Old Fashioned.

13. Proofing down: Proofing down is the process of reducing the alcohol content of a spirit by adding water. This is done to achieve a desired proof or to balance the flavors of the drink.

14. Distillation: Distillation is the process of purifying a liquid by heating it to create steam and then cooling it to collect the condensed vapor. This is how spirits are made, as the alcohol separates from other impurities and is collected in a separate container.

15. Craft spirits: Craft spirits are produced by small, independent distilleries using traditional methods and high-quality ingredients. These spirits often have unique flavors and are highly sought after by enthusiasts.

Now that you’re familiar with some common liquor lingo, you can confidently navigate the world of spirits and elevate your drinking experience. Whether you’re ordering a drink at a bar or experimenting with cocktail recipes at home, knowing these terms will help you communicate your preferences and appreciate the craftsmanship behind each sip. So raise your glass and toast to expanding your drink vocabulary!
