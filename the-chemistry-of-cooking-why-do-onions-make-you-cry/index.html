The Chemistry of Cooking: Why Do Onions Make You Cry?

3 min · 463 words

Onions are a staple ingredient in countless recipes around the world, adding flavor, aroma, and depth to dishes ranging from soups and stews to stir-fries and salads. However, if you’ve ever chopped onions, you’ve likely experienced the stinging sensation and tears that accompany the process. So, why exactly do onions make you cry? The answer lies in the chemistry of these versatile vegetables.

Onions contain a compound called syn-propanethial-S-oxide, which is released when the onion is cut, crushed, or chopped. This compound is part of a larger group of sulfur-containing compounds known as thiosulfinates. When syn-propanethial-S-oxide comes into contact with the moisture in your eyes, it reacts to form a sulfuric acid. It’s this sulfuric acid that irritates the nerve endings in your eyes, triggering the familiar burning sensation and causing your eyes to tear up in an attempt to flush out the irritant.

But why do onions produce syn-propanethial-S-oxide in the first place? The answer lies in the onion’s natural defense mechanism. Like other members of the allium family, such as garlic and shallots, onions contain sulfur compounds as a defense against pests and pathogens. When the onion is damaged, whether by chopping or biting into it, it releases these compounds as a deterrent to potential threats. In the wild, this defense mechanism helps protect the onion from being eaten by animals or attacked by insects.

Interestingly, not all onions produce the same level of tears-inducing compounds. The pungency of an onion is influenced by its variety, growing conditions, and storage. Generally, the more pungent the onion, the higher the concentration of syn-propanethial-S-oxide it contains, and the more likely it is to make you cry.

So, is there a way to chop onions without shedding tears? While there’s no foolproof method to completely eliminate onion-induced tears, there are several strategies you can try to minimize the effects:

  1. Chill the Onion: Cold temperatures can slow down the release of the tear-inducing compounds. Try refrigerating the onion for about 30 minutes before cutting it.

  2. Cut Under Running Water: Running water can help wash away the irritants before they reach your eyes. Try cutting the onion under a stream of running water or with a bowl of water nearby.

  3. Use a Sharp Knife: A sharp knife causes less damage to the onion’s cells, reducing the release of irritants. Dull knives, on the other hand, crush the cells, releasing more of the tear-inducing compounds.

  4. Cut Quickly: The longer you take to chop an onion, the more time it has to release its tear-inducing compounds. Try to chop the onion as quickly as possible to minimize exposure.

  5. Wear Goggles: Specialized onion-cutting goggles or even regular swimming goggles can create a barrier between your eyes and the irritants, preventing tears.

While chopping onions may bring tears to your eyes, understanding the chemistry behind it can help you minimize the discomfort. So, the next time you reach for an onion, armed with this knowledge, you’ll be better prepared to tackle the task with minimal tears.
