Breaking News, Breaking Jokes: Comedy in Journalism

4 min · 790 words

In today’s world, it’s not uncommon to see comedians and satirists incorporated into the news. Whether it’s through satire news shows like The Daily Show or Late Night with Seth Meyers, or through political cartoons in newspapers, comedy has become a staple in journalism. However, the relationship between comedy and journalism has always been one of tension and complexity, particularly when it comes to breaking news situations.

Comedy and journalism share a common goal: to inform the public. The two have been intertwined since the dawn of journalism itself. There are numerous examples throughout history of satire and humor being used to comment on the news of the day. In the 18th century, Jonathan Swift wrote a satirical essay about the famine in Ireland called A Modest Proposal. In the 20th century, political cartoonists like Herblock and Garry Trudeau used satire to comment on current events. And in recent years, shows like Last Week Tonight with John Oliver and Full Frontal with Samantha Bee have continued this tradition.

One of the biggest challenges when combining comedy and journalism is knowing when to use humor and when to take a serious tone in the face of breaking news. After all, some stories are just too serious to joke about. When tragedy strikes, journalists must be respectful and avoid making light of the situation. However, there are plenty of other stories where comedy can be used effectively.

One example of this is the 2016 U.S. presidential election. The election was a particularly contentious one, and comedians and satirists played an important role in helping the public make sense of the day-to-day chaos. Shows like Saturday Night Live and Late Night with Seth Meyers turned the election into a running joke, using humor to highlight the absurdity of the situation. This not only made the public more informed but also helped to reduce the tension and stress that came with the election.

Comedy and journalism can also be used to target politicians who refuse to take accountability for their actions. In 2015, The Daily Show sent “senior correspondent” Jessica Williams to cover an issue with a city council in Texas. When it turned out that the representatives were not going to be held accountable for their actions, Williams turned to comedy. She summed up the situation by saying “I am not surprised by this, but I am absolutely disappointed” before initiating a slow clap to illustrate her frustration. By doing this, Williams used comedy to convey her disappointment in the political representatives while indirectly praising those who stand up against them.

Of course, comedy and journalism are not always effective when used together. Sometimes the humor can undermine the seriousness of the story, or make light of situations that don’t warrant it. Additionally, when cited (including non-news publications), humor or satire content can lead to confusion and misunderstandings at first glance which will yield negative consequences.

In conclusion, humor and satire can be powerful tools for journalists, especially when covering complex and sensitive issues. While there are certainly times when comedy should not be used, there are also many instances where it can help to make sense of confusing and difficult situations. When used effectively, comedy can serve as a way to not only inform but also entertain and alleviate the stress that comes with being a constant consumer of news, especially when a profound and disastrous situation has broken out. As long as humor and satire are used properly, they can be valuable weapons in the journalist’s arsenal.
